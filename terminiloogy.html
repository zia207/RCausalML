Scientific Terminology for Beginners

This quick glossary explains common scientific and causal-inference terms used in this notebook.

1) Causal Effect

  • Explanation: The change in outcome caused by a treatment (not just correlated with it).
  • Example: If a policy increases the average test score by 5 points, the causal effect is +5.

2) Treatment

  • Explanation: The intervention, policy, or exposure whose effect we want to estimate.
  • Example: In this notebook, repeal = 1 means a state received the policy treatment.

3) Outcome

  • Explanation: The measured result affected by treatment.
  • Example: lnr (logged gonorrhea rate) is the outcome variable.

4) Covariate

  • Explanation: A background variable that may influence treatment assignment and/or outcome.
  • Example: Income, poverty, and age are covariates in the abortion dataset.

5) Heterogeneous Treatment Effect

  • Explanation: The treatment effect is not the same for everyone; it varies by subgroup.
  • Example: A policy may have a stronger effect for younger people than older people.

6) CATE (Conditional Average Treatment Effect)

  • Explanation: The average treatment effect for a specific subgroup defined by covariates.
  • Example: CATE for teens with low income might be +0.12, while high income might be +0.03.

7) Confounding

  • Explanation: A third variable affects both treatment and outcome, creating biased estimates.
  • Example: If poorer states are more likely to adopt a policy and also have higher disease rates, poverty can confound the estimate.

8) Overfitting

  • Explanation: A model learns noise in training data and performs poorly on new data.
  • Example: A very deep tree with tiny leaves fits training data well but gives unstable causal effects on test data.

9) Honest Estimation

  • Explanation: Use one part of data to choose splits and another part to estimate leaf effects.
  • Example: Train structure on 50% of data, then estimate treatment effects on the other 50%.

10) Pruning

  • Explanation: Removing weak branches to simplify the tree and improve generalization.
  • Example: After cross-validation, branches that do not reduce error are cut off.

11) Common Support (Overlap)

  • Explanation: Treated and control units should both exist across covariate ranges.
  • Example: If all high-income states are treated and none are control, causal comparison is weak there.

12) Permutation Test

  • Explanation: A nonparametric test that shuffles treatment labels to build a null distribution.
  • Example: If observed CATE variance is much larger than shuffled-data variance, heterogeneity is likely real.

This glossary introduces key scientific terms used in this notebook with simple examples.

1) Instrument (IV)

  • Explanation: A variable that changes treatment assignment but affects the outcome only through treatment.
  • Example: Distance to a training center influences participation but not wages directly.

2) Endogeneity

  • Explanation: Treatment is correlated with unobserved factors that also affect the outcome.
  • Example: More motivated people may both choose treatment and earn higher income.

3) LATE

  • Explanation: Treatment effect for compliers affected by the instrument.
  • Example: Effect of training among people who attend only when encouraged.

4) Compliers

  • Explanation: Individuals whose treatment behavior changes because of the instrument.
  • Example: People who take a course only if they receive a voucher.

5) Exogeneity

  • Explanation: The instrument is independent of unmeasured outcome determinants.
  • Example: Random encouragement assignment is exogenous.

6) Exclusion Restriction

  • Explanation: The instrument has no direct effect on the outcome.
  • Example: Voucher affects income only through training participation.

7) Heterogeneous Effects

  • Explanation: Effects differ across covariate-defined subgroups.
  • Example: Program gains are larger for younger participants.

8) Honest Splitting

  • Explanation: Use separate data for tree splitting and treatment-effect estimation.
  • Example: Half-sample defines leaves, remaining sample estimates IV effects.

  • Example: A model uses both outcomes to improve precision for each outcome effect.

  • Explanation: LATE varies across covariate-defined subgroups.
  • Example: Education returns differ by region or parental education.

Scientific Terminology for Beginners

This quick glossary explains the core scientific terms used in this notebook in plain language.

1) Potential Outcomes

  • Meaning: For each person, imagine two possible worlds: one where they receive treatment and one where they do not.
  • Example: If a patient gets a drug, one potential outcome is their recovery with the drug, and another potential outcome is their recovery without the drug.

2) Treatment Effect

  • Meaning: The difference between outcome with treatment and outcome without treatment.
  • Example: If expected blood pressure decreases by 8 points with treatment and 3 points without treatment, the treatment effect is 5 points.

3) CATE (Conditional Average Treatment Effect)

  • Meaning: The average treatment effect for a specific subgroup with features (X) (for example, age group or risk profile).
  • Example: The treatment may work better for younger patients than older patients; each subgroup has its own CATE.

4) Confounding

  • Meaning: A third factor affects both treatment assignment and the outcome, creating misleading associations.
  • Example: Sicker patients may be more likely to receive treatment and also more likely to have poor outcomes, even if treatment helps.

5) Propensity Score

  • Meaning: The probability of receiving treatment given covariates (X): (P(T=1X)).
  • Example: If two patients have similar age, health history, and baseline risk, they should have similar propensity scores.

6) Counterfactual

  • Meaning: The unobserved outcome for the treatment status the person did not receive.
  • Example: For a treated person, we observe treatment outcome but not what would have happened under control; that missing value is counterfactual.

7) Overlap (Positivity)

  • Meaning: For each covariate profile, there is a non-zero chance of both treatment and control.
  • Example: If a subgroup always receives treatment and never control, causal comparison for that subgroup is unreliable.

8) Nuisance Models

  • Meaning: Helper models (like outcome models or propensity models) used to estimate the final causal quantity.
  • Example: In DR- or R-learning, we first model (E[YX,T]) and (P(TX)), then combine them to estimate CATE.

9) Cross-Fitting

  • Meaning: A sample-splitting strategy where one part of data fits nuisance models and another part estimates treatment effects.
  • Example: Train propensity/outcome models on fold A and estimate pseudo-outcomes on fold B, then rotate folds.

10) Doubly Robust Estimation

  • Meaning: An estimator is consistent if either the outcome model or the propensity model is correctly specified.
  • Example: Even if the propensity model is imperfect, a correctly specified outcome model can still yield valid effect estimates (and vice versa).

Scientific Terminology for Beginners

Before fitting uplift classifiers, it helps to know the core terminology.

  • Classification outcome: A binary target such as 0/1, no purchase/purchase, or no click/click.
  • Treatment assignment: Indicator of who received the intervention (for example, control vs treatment1).
  • Potential outcomes: The two hypothetical outcomes for each person: one if treated, one if not treated.
  • Individual Treatment Effect (ITE): Difference between those two potential outcomes for one person.
  • CATE (Conditional Average Treatment Effect): Average treatment effect for people with similar features \(X\).
  • Uplift curve / AUUC: Evaluation tools that measure how well the model ranks people by true incremental impact.

Scientific Terminology for Beginners

In regression uplift, outcomes are continuous rather than binary. These terms are used throughout the notebook.

  • Continuous outcome: A numeric target such as revenue, time, cost, or test score.
  • Treatment effect: Difference in expected outcome between treated and control groups.
  • CATE: \(\tau(x)=\mathbb{E}[Y\mid T=1,X=x]-\mathbb{E}[Y\mid T=0,X=x]\), the expected effect for a profile \(x\).
  • T-learner: A method that fits separate outcome models for treatment and control, then subtracts predictions.
  • Interaction tree: A tree that finds subgroups where treatment effects differ significantly.
  • Gain curve: A ranking diagnostic that shows cumulative benefit when targeting high-predicted-uplift units first.

Panel data Repeated observations of the same units over time. “Monthly income records for the same households.”
State dependence Current outcome depends on previous outcome states. “Unemployment today depends on unemployment last quarter.”
Time-varying confounder A confounder that changes over time and affects treatment/outcome. “Health status changes yearly and influences both treatment and costs.”
Sequential ignorability At each time point, treatment is as-if random after conditioning on history. “Given past covariates and outcomes, treatment timing is unconfounded.”

Scientific Terminology for Beginners

Term Simple explanation Beginner example
Sample weight Importance assigned to each observation in estimation. “Underrepresented groups get larger weights in the analysis.”
Inverse Probability Weighting (IPW) Reweights data by inverse treatment probability to balance groups. “If treatment probability is 0.2, that treated unit gets weight 1/0.2=5.”
Weighted ATE Average treatment effect computed with observation weights. “Population-representative effect using survey weights.”
Covariate balance Similar distribution of covariates across treatment groups after weighting. “Age and income look comparable after reweighting.”
Stabilized weights Modified IPW weights to reduce variance from extreme values. “Use bounded weights to avoid one observation dominating results.”
Positivity Every covariate profile has a non-zero chance of each treatment level. “For all X values, both treated and control cases exist.”

Scientific Terminology for Beginners

Term Simple explanation Beginner example
Clustered data Observations grouped in units where errors may be correlated. “Students nested within schools.”
Cluster-robust standard errors Standard errors adjusted for within-cluster correlation. “Do not treat students in same school as independent.”
Intra-cluster correlation (ICC) Similarity of observations within the same cluster. “People in same village have similar outcomes.”
Effective sample size Independent information amount after accounting for clustering. “1,000 rows may behave like only 400 independent units.”
Design effect Variance inflation caused by clustered sampling. “Ignoring clustering gives confidence intervals that are too narrow.”
Multiway clustering Robust errors allowing correlation across multiple cluster dimensions. “Cluster by firm and by year at the same time.”

Scientific Terminology for Beginners

Term Simple explanation Beginner example
Ensemble learner Combines multiple models to improve prediction robustness. “Average random forest and boosting predictions.”
Stacking Learns optimal combination weights for base learners. “Meta-model learns how much to trust each algorithm.”
Bias-variance tradeoff Balance between underfitting and overfitting error. “Simple model has high bias; complex model may have high variance.”
Hyperparameter tuning Searching model settings that improve performance. “Choose tree depth and learning rate by cross-validation.”
Out-of-fold prediction Prediction for each row from models not trained on that row. “Row i is predicted by folds where i was held out.”
Metalearner The model that combines outputs of base learners. “Linear regression on predictions from RF, XGBoost, and Lasso.”

Scientific terminology for beginners

Term Simple explanation Beginner example
Covariate An input feature used to describe each unit. Patient age, blood pressure, and prior diagnosis before treatment.
Confounder A variable that affects both treatment choice and outcome. Older patients are more likely to get intensive care and also have worse outcomes.
Counterfactual The unobserved outcome under the treatment not received. For a treated patient, what would recovery have been under no treatment?
Propensity score Probability of receiving treatment given covariates. A model estimates P(T=1|X)=0.72 for one individual.
ITE (Individual Treatment Effect) Estimated effect for one specific individual. Predicted gain of +3 points for patient A if treated.
ATE (Average Treatment Effect) Mean treatment effect across the population. Across all patients, treatment improves outcome by +1.2 on average.
